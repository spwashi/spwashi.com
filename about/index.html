About

I build readable systems that teach their own structure. Spwashi is the public workbench for that aim: a place where software architecture, learning science, symbolic grammar, narrative ecology, and plain-text publishing can accumulate into one inspectable field. I care about structures that let people think together without needing to surrender ownership, legibility, or wonder.

I dropped out of college while trying to answer a question that still structures my work: Why are we here? At UIUC, I founded a student organization called Student Body for Meaningful Education. SBME had three committees — Discussion, Connection, and Create — because I wanted to treat student motivation as a design problem, not just a compliance problem. I wondered whether student council could function as the head of student motivation, and whether a school could become more conscious of the relationship between structure and meaning.

That question did not leave when I left school. It changed substrates. It moved into software, interfaces, note systems, parsers, and educational media. I still care about motivation, but now I think of it as a problem of <legible containment>: how we shape spaces where attention can gather, where internal and external structure can resonate, and where learning can become cumulative without becoming dead.

An important update to the present moment is that my Obsidian application has moved into the review phase. Whether the applicant pool is hundreds or something closer to a ritual number like 1200, I want to answer that pressure with care. So I am treating this season as an invitation to bless the process through quality: 1200 acts of care, 1200 openings for study, 1200 chances to turn attention into form. Not a shallow stunt — a discipline of distributed sincerity.

Philosophy Register

Software as applied linguistic integration

I do not experience software merely as implementation. I experience it as a way of stabilizing shared meaning across time, groups, and substrates. A codebase is a language environment. A UI is a grammar of permission and orientation. A parser is not just a machine that accepts strings — it is a gatekeeper for what kinds of interpretation a community can sustain together.

This is why I keep returning to plain text. Plain text gives us the strongest baseline for shared inspectability, versioning, portability, and long-horizon continuity.

Learning science as engineering discipline

My academic background in learning and education studies taught me to care about more than correctness. It taught me to care about transfer, working memory, motivation, retention, timing, perceptual scaffolding, and the difference between a structure that merely functions and one that actually helps people learn. That difference matters to me everywhere: in a parser error, in a mode switch, in a service surface, in a note-taking environment, in a lore engine, in a recipe.

Readable systems over opaque automation

The AI era does not make structure less important. It makes structure more important. When generation is cheap, the value of legibility goes up. The structures that matter are the ones that let people inspect what happened, recover what they meant, and navigate the relationship between intention and execution without becoming dependent on black boxes. I want systems that remain navigable even when the surrounding technological conditions become more volatile.

Why meaning still structures the work

SBME was my early attempt to give institutional form to the question “Why are we here?” I still think that is the right question. It just shows up now in different clothes: why do we store this locally, why does this parser choose this interpretation, why does this interaction reward one stance and not another, why does a syntax feel alive instead of dead, why does a project deserve time and care?

I sometimes wonder whether octopi really have heads distinct from their bodies, or whether some forms of intelligence are more distributed than our metaphors allow. I think about my companion Boof and the strange beauty of a mind that seems near-harmonious with its own experience. Those reflections matter to the work because they keep me from building systems that assume cognition is merely hierarchical, merely abstract, or merely verbal. Sometimes the healthiest structure is one where mentality and embodiment are less split.

Career and Practice

Experience and trajectory

I have spent roughly fifteen years building software, with a long arc through frontend systems, design systems, developer experience, content surfaces, and public-facing product architecture. My orientation is strongest where engineering, interface design, language, and pedagogy overlap.

Why the dropout matters

I did not leave school because I stopped caring about education. I left because I cared about meaning intensely enough that I could not ignore the mismatch between institutional form and lived motivation. That fracture never stopped being relevant. It is one reason I care so much about environments like Obsidian, where files remain locally owned and where the relationship between structure and thought is not entirely captured by a platform.

How I like to work

I like small, high-taste teams with strong product conviction, technical depth, and a willingness to refine the invisible structure around an experience. I care about architecture, but not architecture for its own sake. I care about the way it changes what a team can sustain, how a product feels to inhabit, and how a community can accumulate trust over time.

What I am aiming toward professionally

I am aiming toward senior, staff, and principal-caliber work that integrates software engineering, design systems, knowledge workflows, and educational structure. I want to help build products that respect local ownership, deep personalization, extensibility, and long-horizon intellectual life.

Systems Register

I build systems

I have spent years building frontend systems, developer tooling, content platforms, and architecture for interfaces that need to stay coherent under real constraints. The most concentrated expression of this right now is spw-workbench: a parser/runtime/tooling environment for Spw and the broader workbench ecosystem.

  • Language tooling and parser-driven systems
  • Interface architecture and design systems
  • Developer experience and workflow design
  • Accessible web applications and content surfaces

Edges I am pushing

I want to deepen the algorithmic and systems knowledge underneath the structures I already use instinctively: parsing, compression, schedulers, concurrency, document integrity, local-first synchronization, rendering, and formal grammar. Pretext physics and brace polarity are part of that effort: ways to make the deep timing and structure of software experientially visible.

  • Data structures underlying text layout and interaction
  • Algorithms for compression and distributed state
  • Formal grammar and computational linguistics
  • Staff-level system architecture and API contracts

Why Obsidian matters to me

Obsidian sits close to several problems I care about deeply: local-first trust, plain-text ownership, metadata-rich files, link-dense knowledge work, extensibility, plugins, and the relationship between visible structure and accumulated thought. I do not merely want to use environments like that — I want to help extend them carefully.

The fit is philosophical as much as technical: systems that preserve user ownership while making the file system more navigable are exactly the kinds of systems I want to help build. Official references: Obsidian Help and Obsidian Developer Docs.

Why readable systems matter

A readable system is not only easier to debug. It is easier to teach, easier to inherit, easier to trust, easier to revise, easier to extend, and easier to mourn if it must end. In an era of proliferating AI surfaces, that matters more. I want structures that let people inspect what happened and understand why.

Land Cluster

The land cluster is a symbolic system for polarity, containment, impulse, and outward signal. Boon and bane define the field, bone carries the structure, bonk marks the event, and honk sends the wave outward. This polarity mirrors the brace physics of the rest of the site.

Mood Field

The palette is weighted across five operators. Tap each node to step its weight. The center shows the resulting color. The page updates live. This is a public way of studying how abstraction can still remain embodied — how structure becomes atmosphere.

The math 
hue = (boon×140) + (bone×40) + (bonk×300) + (honk×200)
sat = 38% + (boon×32%) + (bonk×25%)
light = 52% + (bone×12%) − (bane×18%)

Each operator pulls the hue, saturation, and lightness toward its territory. Bane is the only force that consistently darkens rather than only rotating hue — the constraint that makes the other operators feel heavier.

Pretext, Symmetry, and Flow

I want symbols to behave less like static labels and more like measurable gates. Approaching them from different directions should reveal layers of consideration in the substrate. This is part of what pretext physics is for: allowing the file to remain readable while the surface becomes responsive to stance, rhythm, and containment.

The file remains readable. The surface becomes measurable. The layout learns to breathe. Symbols are not merely static markers; they are gates into different forms of attention. By measuring once and projecting many, a text can remain plain while gaining rhythm, stance, and useful whimsy. This is not decoration for its own sake. It is an attempt to make the relationship between semantics and interaction more observable.

Powered by Pretext.js and the broader Spw workbench effort. This is also one place where I suspect products outside my immediate ecosystem — even sharp-edged tools emerging from the current educational and startup climate — could find something interesting in spw-workbench. The question is not whether education is under strain. The question is what kinds of structures can metabolize that strain into wonder rather than collapse.

Current Direction

The current direction is to align the public work into a durable thread: readable systems, semantic tooling, public publishing, and environments that respect local ownership while expanding what structured interaction can feel like. Formal curricula may eventually live at Spwashi.College, but this site is the workbench and culture starter.

Concretely, that means deepening the technical foundations beneath the aesthetic vocabulary: parser syntax, document integrity, text projection, schedulers, compression, educational interfaces, and the architecture of environments where shared meaning can accumulate over time. It also means strengthening the bridges between the software surfaces and the narrative/publishing surfaces so the whole constellation feels like one field rather than unrelated domains.

I am also treating the present application season as a blessing discipline. If there are 1200 applicants, then let me answer with 1200 distributions of care. If there are 1200 imagined internships, let them be 1200 openings for apprenticeship inside the work: 1200 moments where attention becomes integrity, where process becomes blessing, where an application becomes not just a plea for consideration but evidence of lived structure.

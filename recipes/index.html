Culinary principles as substrate for novel culture.

A recipe is not a procedure — it is a transfer medium. Each principle here is a living structure: it grows differently in different hands, and the differences are the culture.

These pages apply the framework of applied learning science to culinary practice — not to systematize food but to make visible what every cook already knows: that heat, time, salt, and care are operators in a grammar that can be shared, taught, and extended across any discipline. A painter's reduction and a programmer's abstraction are the same gesture wearing different materials.

Substrate Linguistics

The medium carries meaning

Substrate linguistics asks: what does the material know before you begin? Salt has a memory. Sourdough carries its environment. A cast iron pan holds the heat of every meal it has made. The medium is not neutral — it is a prior context that inflects everything added to it.

Grammar before utterance

Before the chef speaks the dish, the kitchen speaks its constraints. Temperature range, available acids, fat solubility — these are the structural operators of culinary grammar. Learning science calls this the enabling environment: the structure that makes the gesture possible.

Culture as accumulated gesture

A sourdough starter is not a recipe — it is a record of every feed, every environment, every hand that has tended it. Novel culture emerges when enough different gestures accumulate in the same substrate: the painter's reduction, the librarian's indexing, the gardener's tending, the engineer's precision, all inoculating the same living medium.

Transfer across disciplines

The deepest recipes transfer. Mise en place is also how you set up a sketchbook, structure a lesson, or open a new codebase. The principle is the same operator wearing different materials. Applied learning science names this far transfer — the most demanding and most generative form of understanding.
